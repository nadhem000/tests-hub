📝 Sample Argumentative Article with Plan

Plan:

  • Introduction – State the topic: technology’s impact on modern life. Present thesis: although technology brings challenges, it has fundamentally made life easier and more comfortable.
  • Arguments for “easier and more comfortable”
    • Instant communication (smartphones, internet) connects people globally.
    • Unlimited access to information and education (online courses, search engines).
    • Entertainment on demand (TV streaming, PlayStation, social media) enriches leisure.
    • Everyday convenience (online banking, e‑commerce, telemedicine) saves time.
  • Counterarguments – ways technology can complicate life
    • Information overload and constant notifications cause stress.
    • Addiction to screens and games reduces physical activity and face‑to‑face interaction.
    • Privacy risks and cyber threats create anxiety.
    • Over‑reliance on devices may weaken basic skills (e.g., mental arithmetic).
  • Rebuttal / Balance – These drawbacks are real but manageable through digital literacy, self‑discipline, and regulation. The benefits far outweigh the downsides when technology is used mindfully.
  • Conclusion – Reaffirm thesis: technology is a powerful tool that, on balance, has made life more comfortable; the key lies in how we harness it.

Article:

Technology: A Double‑Edged Sword That Has Sharpened Our Lives

Few debates are as persistent as the one surrounding technology’s role in our daily existence. Every generation witnesses innovations that promise to revolutionise life, only to be met with warnings about their hidden costs. Today, with smartphones in every pocket and artificial intelligence creeping into every industry, the question is more urgent than ever: has technology truly made our lives easier and more comfortable, or has it introduced complications we never anticipated? After examining both sides, I am convinced that, on balance, technology has immensely improved our comfort and efficiency, even though it demands that we remain vigilant about its pitfalls.

The most obvious benefit of modern technology is the revolution in communication. A few decades ago, a letter would take days to cross borders; today a video call connects a student in Algiers with a relative in Paris instantly. Mobile phones and messaging apps have shrunk the world, allowing families to stay close and businesses to collaborate across continents. This ease of connection reduces loneliness and fosters global understanding – a clear gain in comfort.

Equally transformative is our access to information. The internet is a vast library available 24/7. Students no longer rely solely on textbooks; they can watch lectures from MIT, read scientific papers, or learn a new language through apps. For the curious mind, the web is an endless source of enrichment. In countries like Tunisia and Morocco, young people have used online resources to acquire skills that lead to remote work, lifting themselves and their families out of poverty. Such opportunities were unimaginable thirty years ago.

Entertainment, too, has become richer and more personalised. Television has evolved from a few terrestrial channels to streaming platforms offering thousands of shows on demand. Video game consoles like the PlayStation provide immersive experiences that can be both relaxing and cognitively stimulating. Social media, despite its critics, allows people to share hobbies, find communities, and express creativity. These leisure options make downtime genuinely enjoyable and contribute to mental well‑being when used in moderation.

Convenience in everyday tasks is another realm where technology shines. Online banking means no more queuing at the bank; e‑commerce delivers goods to your door; telemedicine lets you consult a doctor without travelling. Smart home devices can adjust lighting, heating, and even order groceries automatically. All these innovations free up time and mental energy, allowing us to focus on what truly matters – family, personal growth, or simply rest.

Nevertheless, it would be naive to ignore the complications. Information overload is real: we are bombarded with notifications, emails, and news, which can lead to anxiety and a shortened attention span. Many people feel overwhelmed by the sheer volume of content competing for their focus. Moreover, addiction to screens – whether for gaming, scrolling through TikTok, or binge‑watching series – has been linked to sedentary lifestyles and reduced face‑to‑face interaction. Parents worry about children spending hours indoors with a tablet instead of playing outside.

Privacy has also become a major concern. Every click, search, and purchase is tracked by corporations and, potentially, by governments. Data breaches expose intimate details, and the feeling of being constantly watched can be unsettling. Additionally, over‑reliance on technology may erode certain skills: many young people admit they cannot perform simple arithmetic without a calculator or navigate a new city without GPS.

Yet these problems, while serious, are not insurmountable. They call for education and self‑regulation rather than Luddite rejection. Digital literacy programmes can teach children (and adults) to manage screen time, recognise misinformation, and protect their privacy. Technology itself offers solutions: focus modes on phones, parental controls, and apps that track usage. The same tools that distract can also help us build healthier habits.

Furthermore, many of the complications are the growing pains of a rapidly changing world. As societies adapt, norms and regulations evolve. The European Union’s GDPR is a step toward safeguarding privacy; schools are integrating coding and critical thinking into curricula. In the workplace, automation may displace some jobs but also creates new ones that require human creativity and empathy – skills machines cannot replicate. History shows that humanity has always adapted to technological shifts, and there is no reason to believe we will fail this time.

Consider also the role of technology in addressing global challenges. During the COVID‑19 pandemic, it was technology that enabled millions to work from home, students to attend virtual classes, and researchers to develop vaccines in record time. Without high‑speed internet, collaborative platforms, and supercomputers, the world would have ground to a halt. This crisis demonstrated that technology is not merely a convenience but a lifeline.

In the realm of health, wearable devices now monitor heart rates, sleep patterns, and activity levels, empowering individuals to take charge of their well‑being. Telemedicine has brought specialists to remote villages. Prosthetics controlled by neural interfaces and 3D‑printed organs are no longer science fiction. These advances directly translate into longer, healthier, and more comfortable lives.

On a more personal level, think about how technology nurtures creativity. A teenager with a laptop can compose music, edit films, or write a blog that reaches thousands. Platforms like YouTube and TikTok have launched careers that never existed before. The democratisation of content creation gives voice to the marginalised and fosters cultural exchange. This is a profound enrichment of the human experience.

Of course, the digital divide means not everyone enjoys these benefits equally. Millions still lack reliable internet access or affordable devices. This inequality is a serious issue that governments and organisations must address. But the existence of a divide does not invalidate the benefits for those who are connected; rather, it highlights the need to bridge the gap so that more people can share in the comfort that technology provides.

Returning to the examples mentioned in the prompt – TV, internet, mobile phones, PlayStation – each has its critics. Television is often accused of promoting passivity, yet it also educates through documentaries and news. The internet can be a rabbit hole of distraction, but it is also the greatest repository of knowledge ever created. Mobile phones may interrupt conversations, yet they also enable instant help in emergencies. PlayStation and gaming can become obsessive, but they also improve hand‑eye coordination and problem‑solving skills. The dual nature of these technologies reinforces the idea that their impact depends largely on how we use them.

In my own life, I have experienced both the advantages and the downsides. I rely on my smartphone for navigation, communication, and banking, saving hours each week. Yet I have also felt the pull of endless scrolling and had to consciously set boundaries. The difference lies in mindfulness. When I use technology with intention – to learn, to connect, to create – it enriches my existence. When I use it passively, it can drain my time and energy. This personal lesson echoes the broader truth: technology is a tool, not a master.

Looking ahead, emerging technologies such as artificial intelligence, virtual reality, and renewable energy systems promise even greater comforts. AI can personalise education, diagnose diseases earlier, and automate tedious chores. Virtual reality could revolutionise training and tourism. Clean energy technologies may mitigate climate change and ensure a habitable planet. These are not minor improvements; they are leaps toward a future where human potential can flourish.

Nevertheless, we must remain critical and proactive. Ethical frameworks must guide the development of AI to prevent bias and job displacement. Privacy laws need constant updating. Digital well‑being should be part of school curricula. By actively shaping the evolution of technology, we can maximise its benefits and minimise its harms. The choice is not between embracing or rejecting technology; it is about steering it wisely.

In conclusion, while technology undoubtedly introduces new challenges – information overload, addiction, privacy risks – its overall contribution to human comfort and ease is undeniable. It has connected us, educated us, entertained us, and simplified countless tasks. The complications are real, but they are manageable through education, regulation, and personal discipline. Just as fire warmed our ancestors but required respect, technology warms modern life but demands wisdom. Used thoughtfully, it remains one of the greatest forces for comfort and progress the world has ever known.
