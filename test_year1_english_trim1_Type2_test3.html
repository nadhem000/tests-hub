Reading Passage

Internet Addiction: A Personal Story

Paragraph 1: I'm Ryan. I'm 19. I'm addicted to information technology and gaming, so that's any form of electronic gaming or the pursuit of information without purpose. I've always been interested in video games, from a very young age. It never really started to get bad for me until middle school and into high school. As time progressed, it just continued to get worse and worse. It started to affect my school grades, interpersonal relationships, and things of that nature.

Paragraph 2: Then I went off to college, planning on being very successful, and that's when my gaming addiction kicked in. Towards the end of my first quarter, it got really bad. In my second quarter, I didn't go to any classes. I just spent the entire time on the computer. My lowest point was when I realized what I had been doing, that I was on the track to flunk out of college. This would result in me being homeless because my parents had said they wouldn't support me if I didn't succeed in college. Unable to find a way out of this mess, they were very worried. At that point, I just decided something had to change.

Paragraph 3: People don't take internet addiction seriously at all, and it's extremely hard to have it accepted. I'm in a program called The reSTART Internet and Technology Recovery Program. You have to go to 12-step meetings and talk to people in your recovery group, and even other recovering addicts don't accept it as a problem. They are always in denial. Internet addiction is becoming more commonly accepted, but there's still a huge culture of 'It's not a problem, just turn off the computer' sort of mentality. It's not that simple.

distractionpodcast, August 29th, 2016 (Adapted)

Vocabulary

addicted: unable to stop using something harmful

flunk out: to fail and have to leave school or college

interpersonal relationships: relationships between people

denial: refusal to accept the truth

mentality: way of thinking

Task 2: Correction:

Sample email:

Subject: Some advice for your upcoming exam

Dear [Friend's Name],

I heard you're feeling scared and worried about the exam in two weeks, and I wanted to share some tips on how to make revision more enjoyable. First, try to create a study schedule that includes regular breaks - this prevents burnout and keeps your mind fresh. Instead of just reading notes, use different methods like flashcards, mind maps, or teaching the material to someone else.

Make your study environment pleasant with good lighting, comfortable seating, and perhaps some background music if it helps you concentrate. Reward yourself after completing each study session with something you enjoy, like a favorite snack or a short walk.

Remember that understanding concepts is more important than memorizing facts. Try to connect what you're learning to real-life examples or your personal interests. If certain topics seem boring, challenge yourself to find at least one interesting aspect about them.

Most importantly, maintain a positive attitude. Visualize yourself succeeding in the exam. You've prepared well, and you have the knowledge needed. Believe in yourself!

Wishing you the best of luck with your studies. You've got this!

Best regards,
[Your Name]

Note: A good email should have appropriate formatting (greeting, body paragraphs, closing), address the task directly (advising how to make revision a pleasure), use persuasive language, and maintain a friendly, supportive tone throughout.
