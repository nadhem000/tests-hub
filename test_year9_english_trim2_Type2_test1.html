III- Writing - Planning and example

Planning (narrative descriptive article)

  • Headline: Catchy title (e.g., "Smoking: A Dangerous Habit You Must Quit")
  • Introduction: Hook: alarming statistics or personal story. State purpose: to inform about dangers and encourage quitting.
  • Body - Dangers of smoking: Health risks (lung cancer, heart disease, respiratory issues), financial cost, social rejection, addiction, environmental pollution (cigarette butts).
  • Body - Benefits of quitting: Improved health, more money, better smell, longer life, being a role model.
  • Call to action: Encourage readers to quit, seek help, join support groups.
  • Conclusion: Reiterate message, hopeful tone.

Sample article (30 lines)

Smoking: A Dangerous Habit You Must Quit

Did you know that tobacco kills more than 8 million people each year worldwide? Every puff of a cigarette brings you closer to disease and death. As a student concerned about our future, I decided to write this article to open your eyes to the devastating effects of smoking and to urge every smoker to quit today.

First of all, smoking destroys your health. Cigarettes contain over 7,000 chemicals, many of which are toxic. They cause lung cancer, heart disease, stroke, and chronic bronchitis. Smokers often suffer from shortness of breath, persistent cough, and weakened immune systems. Second-hand smoke is also dangerous for family and friends, especially children.

Besides health, smoking burns a hole in your pocket. Imagine spending hundreds of dinars each year on something that slowly kills you. That money could be used for better food, hobbies, or saving for the future. Moreover, smoking makes you smell bad, turns your teeth yellow, and gives you premature wrinkles. Non-smokers often avoid being around smokers because of the unpleasant odour.

Another major danger is addiction. Nicotine is as addictive as heroin. Once you start, it is very hard to stop. Many young people try a cigarette out of curiosity and end up trapped for life. They feel irritable, anxious, and unable to concentrate without a cigarette.

But the good news is: quitting is possible and the benefits start immediately. Within 20 minutes, your heart rate drops. Within a year, your risk of heart disease is halved. You will have more energy, better skin, and extra money. You will also protect your loved ones from passive smoking.

So what are you waiting for? Talk to your doctor, join a support group, or use nicotine patches. Tell your friends and family about your decision – they will support you. Remember, it is never too late to quit. Choose life, not tobacco. Let's make our school a smoke-free zone and inspire others to do the same.

Together, we can fight smoking and build a healthier generation!
