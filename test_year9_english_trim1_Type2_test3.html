I. Reading Comprehension - Correction

1. Correction:

b. emotional feelings

Detailed Explanation:
Throughout the text, Sarah focuses on describing her internal emotions and feelings rather than external events or other people's actions. She mentions being "really nervous," feeling like she had to perform to be liked, and her emotional response to the situation. While she does mention the reactions of others (friends laughing, teacher introducing her), the primary focus is on her own emotional experience - her nervousness, her need to make an impression, and her feelings about being in a new school. The text is written from a first-person perspective emphasizing personal emotions.

2. Correction:

a) Sarah studied in her last school for five years.

Detailed Explanation:
In the first paragraph, the text explicitly states: "The longest time I ever spent was in my last school in England which was for five years." This is a direct answer found in the text. The question asks specifically about her "last school," which is distinguished from the other schools she attended (seven different schools in total).

b) The pupils were laughing naughtily when the teacher introduced Sarah.

Detailed Explanation:
In paragraph 2, after the teacher introduces Sarah and asks the class to make her welcome, the text states: "All it did was have the opposite effect. The kids were laughing naughtily." This indicates that instead of welcoming her warmly, the students reacted by laughing in a mischievous or unkind way. The teacher's introduction had the opposite of the intended effect.

3. Correction:

c. looking

Detailed Explanation:
The phrase "staring at" means looking fixedly or intently at someone or something for a long time. In the context of the text, when Sarah entered the classroom, all the students were staring at her - looking at her intently because she was the new student. While "smiling" and "laughing" are facial expressions, "staring" specifically refers to the act of looking. The text later mentions the students were laughing, but the underlined phrase specifically refers to the act of looking intently.

Sample Email Reply:

Your Reply as the Editor
To: Sam, 14, UK

Hello! How are you?

Thank you for reaching out to Teens Magazine with your concern. I understand how frustrating it can be when you feel your allowance isn't enough for everything you want.

First, let's consider your parents' perspective. $20 a week might be what they can reasonably afford, or they might be trying to teach you financial responsibility. Many parents use allowances to help teenagers learn money management.

Instead of arguing, try having a calm conversation with them. Explain what you need the money for and ask if there are ways you could earn extra through chores or helping around the house.

Here are some practical suggestions:

1. Create a budget: List your expenses and prioritize what's most important.

2. Look for part-time work: Consider babysitting, pet-sitting, or helping neighbors with tasks.

3. Discuss chores for extra pay: Propose specific household tasks beyond your regular responsibilities.

4. Be patient: Building trust takes time, and showing responsibility with what you have can lead to more later.

Remember, your relationship with your parents is more important than money. Approach them respectfully, and you might be surprised by their willingness to help find a solution.

Best wishes,
The Editor
