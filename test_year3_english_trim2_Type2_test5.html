Text: Bring Your Own Technology (BYOT) at PAHS

(1) Phoenixville Area High School is doing everything it can to provide its students with the best possible opportunities. You have probably heard about the newest technology policy at PAHS. The BYOT policy was rolled out and announced with a start date of January 23, following midterm exams.

(2) BYOT stands for Bring Your Own Technology. Under this policy, students are permitted to bring their own technology to school. Students can bring anything from laptops, to iPads. The students are allowed to use their devices in the cafeteria at lunch, before and after school. As long as this new policy is not abused, this is a fantastic opportunity for the student. Junior Sara Gilbert is very enthusiastic: "It's really nice to be able to work on something I've already worked on at home and make finishing touches in school."

(3) Students can now use their devices to take notes, organize assignments, write papers, and have Internet access on a device that is familiar to them. If you do not own these resources or do not wish to bring them in to school, you are not at a disadvantage by any means. Students will still be able to use the desktops in the computer labs. Emails will also be sent to parents to confirm their consent and then the students will be able to have their devices hooked up to the server.

(4) At this point, many of you are probably thinking that this is great, but what will happen if my laptop or iPad, etc. gets stolen? Won't students be distracted in class by sites like Facebook, Twitter, and games? Regarding the devices being stolen, the school principals will assist in finding the thief and attempting to get the devices back but they will not be responsible for reimbursing the students if their devices get stolen. On the subject of logging on to entertaining sites during class time, this will not be an issue. The devices brought into school by the students will go through the same servers and filters as the computers currently at PAHS and the students will not be able to communicate peer-to-peer.

(5) You can register to bring your devices into school. The way to do this is to log onto the website and fill out a form to gain access. Dr. Parkinson said, "An excellent opportunity for our students. We're trying to treat our them like adults and give them opportunities that they will have in college and in the future."

Task 2 – Planning for argumentative article: "Pollution threatening our school"

  • Introduction: Present the problem – pollution is affecting our school environment (litter, exhaust fumes, waste).
  • Body paragraph 1 – Description: Describe visible pollution: plastic waste in yard, smelly bins, noisy traffic, dirty walls.
  • Body paragraph 2 – Dangers: Health issues (allergies, asthma), unpleasant atmosphere, harm to plants and animals, impact on learning.
  • Body paragraph 3 – Advice/Actions: Organise clean-up days, recycling bins, use eco-friendly transport, plant trees, raise awareness.
  • Conclusion: Small efforts by each student can make our school cleaner and healthier; we owe it to ourselves and future generations.

Argumentative article (30 lines approx.)

Our school, once a place of pride, is now suffocating under the weight of pollution. Walk through the courtyard and you'll see crisp packets drifting like fallen leaves, plastic bottles piled near overﬂowing bins, and walls stained by exhaust fumes from the nearby road. The air, especially during rush hours, smells of burnt fuel, making us cough as we hurry to class. This is the reality we face every day – a threat that not only disfigures our environment but also endangers our health. The dangers are real and immediate. Many students complain of headaches, allergies, and respiratory issues, which doctors link to poor air quality. The constant noise from traffic disrupts our concentration during lessons. Birds and small animals that once visited our garden have disappeared. Moreover, the sight of litter demoralises us and gives visitors a negative impression of our school. If we do nothing, this will only get worse, affecting our well-being and our ability to learn. But we can change this. It starts with each of us. First, let's organise a monthly "Clean & Green" day where students and teachers pick up litter and sort recyclables. We can place separate bins for plastic, paper, and organic waste and encourage everyone to use them. Instead of asking parents to drive us, we could walk, cycle, or use the school bus, reducing traffic fumes. Why not start a gardening club to plant trees and flowers along the fence? They absorb carbon dioxide and beautify our surroundings. We could also create posters and announcements to remind peers to dispose of trash properly and avoid single-use plastics. Imagine a school where the air is fresh, the yard is spotless, and nature thrives around us. That future is possible if we act now. Let's not wait for others – let's be the change. Our health, our learning, and our planet depend on it. Together, we can turn our school into a model of environmental responsibility.
