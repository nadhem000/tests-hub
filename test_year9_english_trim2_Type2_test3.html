Text: A life with smoking

(1) My name is Kim. I was born in 1957, in Canada. I was born to two wonderful parents who were both smokers. I started smoking at the age of 12, stealing cigarettes from my parents and stealing packs from the drugstore. I became a nicotine addict at an early age.

(2) My grandfather had always a cigarette, a cigar or a pipe in his mouth and lived to the ripe old age of 90. My aunt, who is 76, has smoked for over 50 years and is still strong today.

(3) I tried to quit so many times; at the age of 24, I quit for a month. Then, when I was 33, I quit again. This time it lasted one year and a half. I didn't really want to quit! I look at my grandfather, at my aunt, at my parents. They have no ill-effects. Nothing is going to happen to me! I'm not a heavy smoker.

(4) In March 2002, I went to the hospital to do some chest X-ray. My doctor said there was a spot on my left lung. It was cancer. It was very close to the heart. The only alternative I had to beat this, was to have my left lung removed. The next month, I had all types of tests. Then I was ready for the surgery. After a 5-hour operation, doctors removed my left lung.

(5) In the beginning, it was difficult for me to breathe. I couldn't lift anything heavy. I had to do things slowly or my heart would start to beat fast. It took me months before I recovered. (To recover = to get well again after an illness)

III- Writing (6 marks)

Task : Your best friend is becoming addicted to the Internet. Send him an e-mail to warn him against the dangers of such addiction and to give him advice on how to limit his use of the Internet.

Detailed correction

I- Reading Comprehension

1- c- The negative effects of smoking (the whole story shows how smoking led to lung cancer).

2- a/ False: Kim's family did NOT suffer from health problems; text: "My grandfather lived to 90", "my aunt is still strong", "They have no ill-effects." b/ False: Because of smoking, Kim had lung cancer, not heart disease (he had a spot on lung, lung removed).

3- miserable (he suffered from cancer, had lung removed, struggled to breathe).

4- b/ Obligation in the past ("had to" expresses obligation).

5- "This" refers to the fact that he had a spot on his left lung / cancer diagnosis.

II- Language

Exercise 1: 1 time-saving, 2 prices, 3 However, 4 secure, 5 Hackers. (Extra: must, ought)

Exercise 2: have been, trip, yet, are, book.

Exercise 3: 1→e, 2→b, 3→d, 4→a, 5→c. (f is extra)

III- Writing - Planning and sample (argumentative / narrative email)

Planning (structure of the email)

  • Opening (greeting & reason): Dear [friend's name], I hope you're well. I'm writing because I'm worried about your internet use.
  • Dangers of internet addiction (argumentative part):
    • Health: eye strain, lack of sleep, sedentary lifestyle.
    • Social: isolation, less real-life interaction.
    • Academic: lower grades, procrastination.
  • Advice to limit use (practical tips):
    • Set time limits / use apps.
    • Find hobbies (sports, reading).
    • Spend time with family/friends offline.
    • Turn off notifications.
  • Closing (encouragement): I'm here for you. Let's meet soon. Take care, [Your name].

Sample email (30 lines)

To: friend@email.com
Subject: Let's talk about your internet use

Dear Alex,

How are you? I hope you're doing well. I'm writing this email because I've noticed that you spend a lot of time online lately, and I'm a bit worried about you. I know the internet is fun and useful, but too much of it can be harmful. I wanted to share some thoughts and advice with you, as a true friend.

First, let me tell you about the dangers of internet addiction. Spending hours every day scrolling through social media or playing online games can affect your health. You might get headaches, eye strain, or even back pain because you sit for too long. Also, if you stay up late at night surfing the web, you won't sleep enough, and that can make you tired and irritable during the day.

Besides health problems, internet addiction can harm your social life. When you're always online, you miss out on real conversations and fun moments with family and friends. You might feel lonely even though you're connected to many people virtually. I've also noticed that your schoolwork seems to suffer — you used to get good marks, but now you often talk about unfinished homework. The internet can be a huge distraction.

But don't worry, there are ways to control your internet use. Here are some tips that might help. First, set a daily time limit for yourself. You can use apps that block certain sites after a few hours. Second, find new hobbies that don't involve screens, like playing football, drawing, or reading adventure books. Third, spend more time with your family or invite me over — we can go to the park or just chat like we used to. Also, try to turn off notifications so you're not tempted to check your phone every five minutes.

I'm not saying you should quit the internet completely, but it's important to find a balance. Remember, I'm always here to support you. Maybe we can make a plan together to spend less time online and more time doing fun things in real life. Let me know what you think, and let's meet this weekend, okay?

Take care and write back soon.

Yours,
Jamie
