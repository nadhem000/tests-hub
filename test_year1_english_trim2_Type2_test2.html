Text: Henry's £4.5 million Secret

The villagers in Middleton, Yorkshire often worried about poor old Henry Wardle. Henry, 68, lived alone except for his ancient cat, Tiddles, in a tiny, one-bedroom cottage, and always asked for credit when buying cat food in the local shop. Then last month Henry died leaving 4.5 million pounds in his will. The truth was that Henry was a multi-millionaire, and he owned houses all over the country from which he made a fortune in rent. All this came as a complete shock to his neighbours. They believed that Henry was a poor window cleaner and felt sorry for him, and indeed he often entertained them with tales from his window-cleaning days.

However, his brother, Mr Sam Wardle, 82, said that this was all nonsense. Henry had never been a window cleaner but had started work at the age of fourteen as a bricklayer. Then when he was 30, he bought his first house, and after that he frequently bought and sold houses. Sam says that his brother's only interest was making money, but he hated spending it. He didn't spend a penny of his own money unless he had to. Henry never had a holiday, but for many years he had a girlfriend, a lady called Betty Barraclough, but he decided not to marry her because a wife was too expensive.

Henry left his £4,500,000 to Tiddles and a local cat charity. His friends and family received nothing.

Planning for argumentative article: "Do good neighbours still exist?"

  • Introduction: State the topic – neighbourhood relations have changed, but good neighbours still exist. Thesis: good neighbours are rare but valuable.
  • Body paragraph 1 – qualities of good neighbours: helpful, respectful, friendly, discreet. Examples: helping in emergencies, lending tools.
  • Body paragraph 2 – modern challenges: busy lives, urbanization, anonymity. But many still form bonds.
  • Body paragraph 3 – personal example: describe a neighbour who helped or a community event.
  • Conclusion: Good neighbours exist if we also strive to be good neighbours; mutual effort builds community.

Argumentative article (30 lines)

In today's fast-paced world, many people feel that the sense of community has faded. However, I firmly believe that good neighbours still exist, and their presence can greatly enhance our daily lives. A good neighbour is not just someone who lives next door, but a person who embodies kindness, respect, and solidarity. First and foremost, good neighbours are those who offer help without expecting anything in return. For instance, when my family was away, our neighbour Mr. Smith watered our plants and kept an eye on our house. Such small gestures build trust and create a safe environment. Moreover, good neighbours respect privacy while being approachable. They greet you warmly and are ready to lend a hand during emergencies, like a power cut or a sudden illness. Personal experience has taught me that good neighbours are often the ones who make a house a home. When my family moved into our current home five years ago, we were strangers. Within a week, a neighbour brought us a homemade pie and a list of reliable local tradespeople. That simple welcome broke the ice and led to lasting friendships. Another neighbour, a young father, started a Saturday morning football game in the park that now involves children from a dozen families. These interactions are not only pleasant; they build a support system that can be relied upon in times of crisis. During the recent heatwave, several neighbours checked on the elderly residents, ensuring they had water and working fans. Such vigilance is priceless. Nevertheless, it is true that modern life, with its long working hours and digital distractions, makes it harder to foster neighbourly ties. In big cities, people often ignore those living next door. Yet, this does not mean good neighbours have vanished. In my own neighbourhood, we have a community WhatsApp group where we share news and offer support. Last month, we organized a street party that brought everyone together, proving that neighbourliness can thrive. In conclusion, good neighbours do exist, but they require effort from both sides. By being open, helpful, and considerate, we can cultivate relationships that make our streets friendlier and our lives happier. After all, a good neighbour is a treasure worth having.
