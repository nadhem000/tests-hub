Text: How Well Do You Know Your Kids?

[1] In survey after survey, many Kids say that they feel increasingly lonely, unable to connect with their parents, teachers and sometimes even classmates. They are in great need of guidance, and when they don't get what they need at home or school, they immerse themselves in a universe out of their parents' reach, a world defined by computer games, TV and movies, where brutality and violence are so common.

[2] In fact, of all the problems that trouble adolescents, loneliness ranks at the top of the list. University of Chicago sociologist Barbara Schneider has been studying 7,000 teenagers for five years and has found that they spend an average of 3 1/2 hours alone every day. Teenagers may claim they want privacy, but they also crave and need attention- and they're not getting it. In the teenage years, the relationship between parents and children is constantly evolving as the kids edge toward independence. Early adolescence is a period of transition, when primary school kids move from one teacher and one classroom to a different teacher for each subject. In puberty, they're moody and irritable. "This is a time when parents and kids argue a lot."

[3] When they are isolated from parents, teens are also more vulnerable to serious emotional problems. Surveys of high-school students have indicated that one in four consider suicide each year, says Dr. David Fassler, a child and adolescent psychiatrist in Burlington.

[4] In the teenage years, parents should try to understand what their children are going through. Parents shouldn't force their children to be something they want them to be, but help their children to be who they are. Finally, parents should seize any opportunity to talk to them- in the car, over the breakfast table and watching TV.

1- Ibn Khaldoun – sample biography

Ibn Khaldoun, a famous historian and sociologist, was born in Tunis in 1332. He studied at Zeytouna University where he received certification in linguistics, mathematics, and logic. In 1356, he moved to Fez, Morocco, to write the history of its king Sultan Mohamed. Later, in 1377, he wrote his masterpiece "Muqaddimah". Sample article (≈30 lines)

**Is the Internet stealing our lives?**

Have you noticed how our breaks are now silent? Everyone is staring at a screen. We spend hours online, but is it really harmless? As a student, I see classmates constantly connected – during lunch, in the corridors, even while walking. The internet is a wonderful tool, but overusing it brings serious drawbacks that we must talk about.

First, the more time we spend online, the less we interact in real life. We prefer texting to talking, and genuine friendships suffer. Loneliness increases despite being virtually connected. Second, our health pays the price: strained eyes, headaches, and a sedentary lifestyle lead to obesity. Many of us sleep with our phones, disrupting our sleep cycle and making us tired at school.

Academically, the situation is alarming. Notifications constantly interrupt our study time. We find it hard to focus on a book for ten minutes without checking social media. Grades drop because we replace homework with endless scrolling. Worse, the internet exposes us to cyberbullying, violent content, and misinformation. It's easy to feel anxious or depressed after seeing unrealistic lives on social platforms.

So, what can we do? We don't have to quit the internet entirely, but we need balance. Set time limits, turn off notifications, and rediscover offline hobbies – sports, reading, or just talking with family. Let's use the internet wisely before it uses us.

By [Your Name], 1st year student

Detailed Plan and Model Article (50 lines)

Plan:

  • Title: The Internet: A Blessing or a Curse?
  • Introduction: Hook about the prevalence of internet among classmates; thesis: despite its benefits, overuse leads to serious problems.
  • Body paragraph 1 – Social isolation: Reduced face-to-face interaction, weakening of real friendships, increase in loneliness.
  • Body paragraph 2 – Health issues: Physical: eye strain, sedentary lifestyle, obesity; Mental: anxiety, depression, sleep disorders.
  • Body paragraph 3 – Academic decline: Distraction, procrastination, lower grades, reduced attention span.
  • Body paragraph 4 – Exposure to harmful content: Cyberbullying, violence, misinformation, unrealistic standards.
  • Body paragraph 5 – Addiction and loss of time: Endless scrolling, gaming addiction, neglecting responsibilities.
  • Conclusion: Call for balanced use, practical tips, and a reminder that we control technology, not the other way around.

Article:

**The Internet: A Blessing or a Curse?**

Walk into any classroom during break, and what do you see? Most students are hunched over their phones, scrolling through social media or watching videos. The internet has become an inseparable part of our lives. While it offers incredible opportunities for learning and connection, its overuse among teenagers is causing alarming consequences. As a student, I feel it is my duty to warn my peers about the hidden dangers of excessive internet use.

First and foremost, the internet is paradoxically making us more isolated. We may have hundreds of online friends, but how many real, meaningful conversations do we have? Instead of talking face to face, we send emojis and texts. This lack of genuine interaction weakens our social skills and deepens feelings of loneliness. Studies show that teenagers today report higher levels of loneliness than previous generations, despite being constantly connected.

Secondly, our physical and mental health is suffering. Hours spent staring at screens lead to digital eye strain, headaches, and poor posture. A sedentary lifestyle, combined with snacking while browsing, contributes to obesity and related diseases. Mentally, the constant comparison with curated online lives triggers anxiety and depression. The blue light from screens disrupts our sleep, making us tired and irritable during the day. Many teenagers sacrifice sleep to stay online, creating a vicious cycle of exhaustion and mood swings.

Academically, the impact is equally devastating. Notifications from apps constantly interrupt our concentration. It becomes nearly impossible to read a book or finish homework without checking our phones every few minutes. This fragmented attention reduces our ability to learn deeply and retain information. Grades drop, and we blame ourselves, not realizing that the real culprit is our digital habits. Procrastination becomes a habit, and we end up rushing assignments at the last minute, stressed and anxious.

Furthermore, the internet exposes us to content that can harm our developing minds. Cyberbullying is rampant, and victims often suffer in silence. Violent videos, hate speech, and misinformation spread quickly, shaping our worldview in negative ways. Social media platforms often present unrealistic beauty standards and lifestyles, leading to body image issues and low self-esteem. Without proper guidance, we may accept these false narratives as truth.

Addiction is another serious concern. The internet is designed to be addictive; every like, comment, or notification triggers a dopamine rush. Many of us find ourselves unable to disconnect, even when we want to. We waste hours on meaningless content, neglecting hobbies, exercise, and time with family. This addiction can escalate to gaming disorders or compulsive use of social media, which are now recognized as mental health conditions.

So, what can we do to protect ourselves? The solution is not to abandon the internet entirely, but to use it mindfully. We can start by setting daily time limits for non-educational screen use. Turning off non-essential notifications helps reduce distractions. It is also important to create tech-free zones, such as the dining table or bedroom, to encourage real conversations and better sleep. Rediscovering offline activities – sports, reading, art, or simply spending time in nature – can restore balance. Parents and teachers should also guide us in developing healthy digital habits.

In conclusion, the internet is a powerful tool, but like any tool, it must be used wisely. We are the generation that grew up with smartphones, and we have the responsibility to shape a healthy relationship with technology. Let's not allow the internet to steal our youth, our health, and our future. Instead, let's use it to enhance our lives without letting it control us. Remember, we are the ones in charge.

By [Your Name], 1st year student
