Text: Online school reporting systems

(1) The end of the traditional school report will soon be announced as ministers plan to give parents daily electronic access to their children's school records.

(2) All schools will be expected to set up 'real-time reporting' systems that will allow parents to see their children's attendance records, grades and discipline reports. The information could be made online or via e-mails, text messages or even teleconferencing, which will make it easier for parents to be regularly involved in their children's education and will improve school standards. "The systems will be more efficient and frequent than a once-a-year written report, or a letter home when there is a problem or something to celebrate", said Mr. Knight, the schools minister.

(3) But security experts warned that any weaknesses in the Information Technologies systems opened up the risk of sensitive data being lost or stolen. Besides, some teachers fear being fed up with demands for information from parents.

(4) "By 2010, all secondary schools should offer real-time reporting systems, with primary schools expected to follow two years later", said Mr. Knight. "All parents will be encouraged to buy a personal computer as it should be as important as going to school with a calculator or pencil case", he added.

(5) The department of Children, Schools and Families will set aside 30 million pounds over the next three years to help low-income families buy computers and connect to the internet. Mr. Knight will make his announcement at a computer industry conference, where he hopes to persuade manufacturers to support the government plan by offering discounted equipment.

(6) The schools minister has promised that the new reporting systems will not add to teachers' workloads, but Mr. Sinnott, the general secretary of the National Union of Teachers said: "there has to be evidence that the real-time reporting systems do not increase teachers' workloads before we can accept them".

(adapted from The Daily Telegraph, Wednesday, January 9, 2008)

Task 1 – Sample paragraph (advice for revising)

To revise well for exams, you should use a marker to highlight important information and summarize long topics. It is also helpful to alternate easy and difficult subjects to keep your mind active. However, avoid revising too much the night before the exam, and don't waste time on things you already know. Following these tips will ensure success.

Task 2 – Argumentative article: planning

  • Introduction: Present the topic – online reporting systems are becoming common. Mention the debate.
  • Advantages: Parents are better informed, quick access to information, improves communication, can boost school standards.
  • Disadvantages: Data security risks, possible increase in teachers' workload, digital divide for low-income families.
  • Your opinion: Balanced view – systems are beneficial if properly implemented with security and training.
  • Conclusion: Summarize and suggest cautious adoption.

Sample article (30 lines)

In recent years, many countries have started introducing online reporting systems that allow parents to access their children's school records in real time. This innovation has sparked a debate: are such systems a blessing or a curse? In this article, I will examine both the advantages and disadvantages before giving my personal viewpoint. On the one hand, real-time reporting offers several benefits. First, it enables parents to monitor their children's attendance, grades, and behaviour on a daily basis. This constant feedback helps them to intervene early if problems arise. Second, schools can communicate more efficiently with families through emails or text messages, replacing the old once-a-year written report. As a result, parents become more involved in their children's education, which can lead to higher motivation and better school standards. Moreover, the use of technology prepares students for a digital future. On the other hand, there are significant drawbacks. Security experts warn that online systems may be vulnerable to hacking, leading to sensitive data being lost or stolen. Furthermore, teachers may feel overwhelmed by constant requests for information from parents, adding to their workload without any reduction in other duties. Another concern is the digital divide: not all families can afford a computer or internet connection, especially those on low incomes. The government in the text allocated funds to help, but this may not be enough in all contexts. In my opinion, online reporting systems are a positive step forward, provided that schools invest in robust cybersecurity and offer training for teachers. It is also essential to ensure that every family has access to the necessary technology. If these conditions are met, the benefits outweigh the risks. Parents will be more engaged, and students may feel more supported. To conclude, real-time reporting has the potential to transform school-family communication, but it must be introduced carefully. Policymakers should listen to teachers' concerns and guarantee data protection. Only then can we fully embrace this digital tool in education.
