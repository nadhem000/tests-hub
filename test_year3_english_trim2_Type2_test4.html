Text: Hurricane Wilma - Cozumel

1- In late October of 2005, as the hurricane season was winding down, we kept one eye on Tropical Storm Wilma as she wandered aimlessly in the open sea. We assumed that sooner or later she would decide to head off, as they all do, to some other place. We monitored with growing concern, as Wilma began to strengthen and head more in our direction. Then, with true alarm, we watched her intensify explosively, in just 24 hours, from a tropical storm to a Category Five hurricane. The most intense storm ever recorded in the Atlantic basin was taking aim on Cozumel. We realized that a megahurricane would be on our doorstep within the next 48 hours.

2- Fortunately here in Cozumel the buildings and houses are constructed to withstand hurricanes. They are built with reinforced concrete and block so there is not the problem of evacuating the entire citizenry. Public refuges were provided for those few whose homes were not safe enough! For safety, the power grid was shut down too; so downed electric cables lying in the street would not be a hazard in the aftermath.

3- Wilma roared in right on schedule, and she howled and pounded us for 65 hours! We were allowed only a brief respite when the eye passed directly over the island. The monster then stormed off to terrorize Cancun leaving Cozumel battered and temporarily out of contact with the rest of the world. We found ourselves without electricity, communication, or running water; and no way off the island. Venturing outside, I saw that the power lines and poles were a tangled spider web lying in the flooded streets, and the normally magnificent greenery bare and broken. The waterfront structures, restaurants and shops essential to most everyone's income, were severely damaged.

4- That night, looking out over the normally vibrant city now pitch- black and silent, one would think that Cozumeleros would have despaired - but they did not. Once it was discovered that everyone was safe, they breathed a collective sigh of relief, and immediately began planning for our recovery.

5- The moment it was over, with black skies and seas still churning, the Cozumeleros hit the streets. Mostly on foot, as the streets were blocked with power poles and debris, they checked on friends and relatives the old- fashioned way. The conversation on every corner was the same - "Did you see so and so? Are they okay? Did their home survive?" We were like tourists in our own town, wandering around gawking at everything, snapping photos and staring in amazement at the visions of ruin before us.

6- But there was no aimless waiting around for help here. The next day a chorus of saws, hammers, brooms and chainsaws could already be heard. The government was out in force too, assessing the damage, distributing supplies and gearing up for the big cleanup. Food packages were delivered door- to- door, one for each citizen, full of the essentials of life in Mexico - rice, tortilla flour, beans, sugar, coffee, and even cookies for the kids.

7- The island was quickly a flurry of activity - demolishing, cleaning, building, fixing, and painting. Pier reconstruction was a hot activity. Most businesses used their own staff to perform repairs, thereby keeping their employees working despite the lack of tourism. Waiters and busboys became painters, divemasters and scuba instructors poured concrete, boat captains and their first mates moonlighted as landscapers. These men and women took great pride in their work, knowing that they themselves were helping to get their regular jobs rolling again, as soon as possible.

Detailed correction

I- Reading Comprehension

2- a) False – Their buildings were strong enough (built with reinforced concrete). b) True – It lasted 65 hours = 2 days and 17 hours.

3- "Did you see so and so?", "Are they okay?", "Did their home survive?" ; "tourists".

4- Meaning 1: to fix the rate or amount of (assessing the damage).

5- During: power lines down, flooded streets, bare greenery, damaged waterfront structures. After: cleanup, saws, hammers, government distributing food, people repairing, businesses using staff for repairs.

6- 1+d, 2+a, 3+b, 4+c (extra e).

Planning for descriptive article: "Tsunami aftermath in Thailand"

  • Introduction: Set the scene – friends' honeymoon, tsunami strikes, loss of contact, decision to travel.
  • Body paragraph 1 – arrival and first sights: Describe the devastation – collapsed buildings, debris, overturned cars, chaos.
  • Body paragraph 2 – human impact: Search for friends, encounters with survivors, relief efforts, emotional scenes.
  • Body paragraph 3 – resolution: Find friends alive (or not), their condition, relief and sorrow.
  • Conclusion: Reflection on the power of nature, resilience of people, importance of preparedness.

Sample article (30 lines)

When I heard that a massive tsunami had struck Thailand, my heart stopped. My best friends, Ahmed and Leila, were there on their honeymoon. For two agonizing days, no phone call, no message. I booked the first flight to Phuket. Stepping off the plane, the airport was a makeshift shelter. Dazed tourists sat on floors, wrapped in blankets. Outside, the world was grey. Palm trees were snapped like twigs, cars piled on top of each other, and a thick layer of mud covered everything. The stench of salt and decay hung in the air. I walked through streets where shops had been gutted, their contents strewn across the road. People wandered aimlessly, calling out names. I joined a group of volunteers searching the hospitals. The first one was overflowing; injured people lay on corridors. I scanned every face – nothing. At the second hospital, a nurse handed me a list of survivors. My hands trembled. There, under "Foreigners", I saw Ahmed's name. I shouted with joy. They were alive. I found them in a small ward. Leila had a broken arm, Ahmed a deep cut on his leg, but they were alive. They told me how they had been swept inland and rescued by locals. The relief was overwhelming. Yet, around us, many families were not so lucky. The tsunami had taken everything. That night, I looked out at the ocean, calm now, hiding its fury. I thought of the thousands who perished and the strength of those who survived. My friends' honeymoon became a nightmare, but their survival was a miracle. We returned home forever changed, grateful for each day.
